Subtractive Synthesis

Subtractive synthesis works by starting with waveforms rich in harmonic content and removing harmonics using filtering. Many early synthesizers, such as the Minimoog, were subtractive synthesis and this remains a common building block for sound synthesis. Wikipedia has a very nice article on subtractive synthesis. You are required to implement the following waves:

  • square waves
  • sawtooth waves
  • triangle waves

Pulse waves are also included as an option. You must implement bandwidth limited waves, so create these waves as tables, NOT mathematically. A simple offline program can generate these waveforms. The method for playing the waves is similar to that of the wavetable synthesis component. Elements of a subtractive sythesizer:

Reson

A Reson is a simple recursive filter that emphasizes a specific frequency. It's a simple solution for filtering in this application. A step project will describe the implementation of a Reson. The instructor can also provide you with additional details for this component. You should accomplish something approximating the "Moog sound".

Polyphony

Polyphony simply means playing more than one note at a time. When you strum a guitar, you are playing six notes at a time. Polyphony means being able to start and stop more than one note at the same time.

Envelope generation

How an instrument starts the sound and sustains it varies, but usually has a volume characteristic to it. An example, a violin may slowly start the sound of a note or quickly start it for different songs. This is called an "attack". Many instruments have a rapid decay after the attack, then sustain at another level, only to gradually decay at the end. In all cases for a wavetable synthesis component you will need to be able to fade the sound at the end over several milliseconds to avoid a "pop". Wikipedia has a good article on ADSR envelopes and we will discuss them in class.

Filter Envelopes

Interesting filter effects are often created by using the same envelope design used for amplitude envelopes to filter parameters. Sweeping the filter frequency creates the famous "Moog" sound.
